\begin{abstract}
Affordances—actions made possible by the features of a space—are fundamental to design. 
Thus, the ability to evaluate the design of a built environment depends on understanding the actions it affords.
Affordance judgments depend on object identity, layout, clearance, and scene
context, but obtaining dense human ratings is expensive. We therefore study a
practical proxy question: how accurately can lightweight student models
reproduce vision-language-model-derived affordance scores on synthetic indoor
scenes?
Using 420 Hypersim renderings held out by scene, we queried Qwen2-VL-7B for 7-point scores and structured indicator checklists across five activities drawn from an expert-curated taxonomy: Sleep, Cook, Computer Work, Casual Conversation, and Yoga/Stretching. Student models were trained on either raw RGB images or structured Mask2Former-derived scene features. On held-out test scenes, an indicator-augmented LightGBM student (Model D) achieved macro RMSE 0.765 and macro Pearson r = 0.724, outperforming a feature-only LightGBM (Model B; RMSE 0.977), the best CNN checkpoint (RMSE 1.120), and a mean-prediction baseline (RMSE 1.154). Model D reduced RMSE on every affordance relative to Model B, with statistically significant gains on four of five tasks; the largest improvement was on Computer Work ($\Delta$ RMSE = −0.328, 95\% bootstrap CI $[−0.500, −0.154]$). A permutation control shows that indicator semantics drive most of this gain on three affordances, though dimensionality also contributes on the remaining two. Under a binary view defined by score ≥ 4, Model D attains the best macro F1 among reproducible baselines (0.679). We present these results as evidence for distillation fidelity: lightweight tabular students can approximate a VLM teacher substantially better than image-only baselines on this synthetic benchmark, though their correlation with human affordance judgments remains untested.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Gibson's theory of affordances describes environments in terms of the actions
they make possible~\cite{Gibson1979}. For indoor scenes, that means a room may
afford sleeping, cooking, working, socializing, or stretching depending on a
combination of furniture and layout. Predicting such judgments from images is
relevant to embodied AI, robotics, and functional scene understanding.

Recent vision-language models (VLMs) can make holistic scene judgments
directly~\cite{Bai2023,Radford2021}, and recent annotation work has shown that
large models can serve as useful proxy labelers in some domains when human
annotation is scarce~\cite{Gilardi2023}. Our paper adopts that paradigm, but
with an explicit caveat: Qwen2-VL-7B is a scalable \emph{proxy annotator}, not
ground truth. Accordingly, our evaluation measures how well lightweight
students reproduce the teacher's outputs, not whether those outputs match human
affordance judgments. Making that distinction explicit is central to the
paper.

Within that scope, we study whether structured VLM supervision is more useful
than scalar targets alone. Qwen2-VL-7B produces both a 1--7 affordance score
and an indicator checklist; the students then attempt to predict the scalar
score from either RGB images or structured scene features. This yields three
questions. First, how well can small students approximate VLM-derived scores on
held-out scenes? Second, do structured features outperform image-only CNN
baselines in this low-data setting? Third, does indicator-level distillation
help beyond the scalar target alone?

On a scene-level split of 420 Hypersim images, structured LightGBM students outperform the best CNN checkpoints and the mean baseline. Adding indicator features improves every affordance and is statistically significant on four of five tasks, though the Yoga/Stretching gain remains uncertain at this sample size. A random-indicator control shows the improvement is not explained purely by adding binary columns. We present this work as an end-to-end distillation pipeline for VLM-derived indoor affordance scoring on synthetic scenes — not as a claim that affordance prediction has been solved. Even a coarse classifier of what an interior appears to afford is practically useful before full human validation: it supports archive-scale queries, comparison of design variants, and fast screening of scenes for deeper review. Affordance scoring is intended to serve exactly that role as one analysis module within a broader pipeline for extracting design-relevant visual, spatial, and cognitive attributes from interior images, which we submit as a supplement 

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

This project sits at the intersection of affordance modeling, distillation, and
model-assisted annotation. Prior affordance work ranged from object-centric
prediction~\cite{Do2018AffordanceNet,Chuang2018} to environment-scale reasoning
in egocentric and 3D indoor settings~\cite{Nagarajan2020,Li2019PuttingHumans}.
Our setting uses continuous room-level suitability scores from a VLM teacher.

The learning setup is response-based distillation~\cite{Hinton2015} with an
additional structured-rationale channel related to broader distillation
surveys~\cite{Gou2021}. Hypersim~\cite{Roberts2021}, Mask2Former~\cite{Cheng2022},
LightGBM~\cite{Ke2017}, and ResNet~\cite{He2016} are established components; the
contribution is their combination into a reproducible scene-level VLM
distillation pipeline. Our annotation strategy also follows work showing that
large models can sometimes serve as useful proxy annotators, while still
requiring careful caveats about construct validity~\cite{Gilardi2023}.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Data, Teacher Labels, and Split Strategy}

The dataset contains 420 \emph{synthetic} Hypersim images sampled across seven
room clusters (bathroom, bedroom, dining room, kitchen, living room,
lobby/lounge, and office), yielding 2,100 image-affordance rows after scoring
each image on five affordances. The target affordances are L059 Sleep, L079
Cook, L091 Computer Work, L130 Casual Conversation, and L141
Yoga/Stretching. Qwen2-VL-7B assigns each image-affordance pair a scalar score
on a 1--7 ordinal scale and emits a structured list of supporting or
contradicting indicators.

\figref{fig:pipeline} summarizes the full workflow. We extracted 310 structured
scene features from Mask2Former segmentations: 133 object-presence features, 133
counts, 15 pairwise 2D distances, 15 pairwise depth differences, and 14
aggregate scene statistics such as free-floor fraction, clutter, and depth
spread. Indicator vocabulary consolidation yielded 624 canonical indicator
types, expanded into 1,248 affordance-specific positive/negative binary columns.

The split is \emph{scene-level} and stratified by room cluster: 224 train
scenes, 32 validation scenes, and 64 test scenes, corresponding to 298/38/84

split prevents leakage through shared geometry and layout.

\subsection{Students and Training Details}

We evaluate five predictors. The \model{Mean} baseline predicts the training-set
mean score for every test image. The \model{Linear} baseline fits ordinary least
squares on the 310 structured features. The \model{Best CNN} row reports the
best retained ResNet-18 or ResNet-50 checkpoint per affordance from the
original training sweep. \model{Model B} is a LightGBM regressor trained on the
310 structured features, and \model{Model D} augments Model B with the
affordance-specific indicator columns.

For LightGBM, we reuse the best per-affordance Optuna configurations from the
original experiments and retrain on the combined train+validation split with
the standard L2 regression objective. The original search used 100 Optuna
trials with 5-fold cross-validation over standard tree-depth, leaf-count,
learning-rate, estimator-count, sampling, and regularization ranges.

This is not a pure architecture comparison between CNNs and gradient boosting.
The CNNs receive raw pixels, whereas the tabular models receive a strong
segmentation-derived representation. The supported claim is therefore that
\emph{structured feature pipelines outperform image-only fine-tuning in this
low-data synthetic regime}, not that boosted trees are inherently superior to
CNNs. The absolute error also remains large
enough to matter: macro RMSE 0.765 is promising for coarse ranking, retrieval,
or triage, but not convincing enough to replace the VLM teacher when
image-level fidelity matters.

\subsection{Thresholded Classification View}

As an auxiliary analysis, we converted the 1--7 score scale into a binary
label: scores $\geq 4$ were treated as ``affords'' and scores $\leq 3$ as
``does not afford.'' \tabref{tab:f1} reports positive-class F1 for the
reproducible per-image prediction vectors. Under this semantically clean
midpoint threshold, Model D remains the strongest overall, with macro F1
0.679. After re-inspecting the boundary examples in \figref{fig:threshold_examples},
we regard this midpoint as the proper semantic cutoff: the score-3 Sleep and
Yoga/Stretching cases are visibly kitchens, so treating them as negatives is
more defensible than calling them weak positives.

We also swept $\{2.5, 3.0, 3.5, 4.0, 4.5, 5.0, 5.5\}$.
\tabref{tab:f1sweep} shows that all reproducible models achieve their best
macro F1 at threshold 2.5, with Model D reaching 0.906. We do not report CNN F1
because only aggregate checkpoint summaries, not per-image prediction vectors,
were retained from the original image-model experiments.

\figref{fig:threshold_examples} explains why the lower threshold performs
better mechanically. In all three examples, the teacher assigns score 3 and
Model D predicts roughly 3.0, so 2.5 preserves them while 4.0 forces them
negative. Visual inspection suggests that the stricter decision is the right
one for interpretation. The implication is not that 2.5 is the better
affordance threshold, but that the students are better calibrated to the
teacher's low-middle score range than to the sharper binary boundary at 4. In
other words, lowering the threshold improves teacher-matching F1 by relaxing
the decision rule, not by producing a better partition.

The permutation control adds an important nuance absent from the original draft.
On L059, L079, and L091, randomly permuting the indicator assignments largely

claim is therefore not that indicator semantics \emph{fully} explain the
gain, but that they explain a substantial fraction of it.

\subsection{Score Diagnostics}

The VLM score distributions help explain both the successes and the limits of
the benchmark. L079 Cook has low entropy (1.19 bits) and a strong floor effect:
74.1\% of all scores equal 1. That compression likely makes canonical kitchens
easy to separate while also reducing the richness of the target distribution.
At the opposite extreme, L059 and L141 have higher entropy and broader support,
which is consistent with their greater dependence on layout and mixed cues.
Inter-affordance correlations are moderate rather than degenerate (maximum
pairwise Pearson $r=0.462$ between L059 and L141), which suggests the VLM is
not simply outputting one generic ``good room'' score across all tasks.

\subsection{Teacher Sanity Checks}

Without human labels, the most we can do is ask whether the teacher partitions
the scenes in an internally plausible way. \figref{fig:teacher} provides two
sanity checks. First, the threshold-4 co-occurrence matrix shows that some
obvious incompatibilities are rare: only 1.7\% of images are positive for both
Sleep and Cook, and 3.1\% for Cook and Yoga/Stretching. Second, conceptually
adjacent activities overlap much more, especially Computer Work and Casual
Conversation (71.9\%), which suggests the teacher often treats lounge-office
scenes as multifunctional rather than sharply separable. The high-confidence
exemplars are visually plausible for all five affordances. These diagnostics
are reassuring enough to justify distillation experiments, but they are still
sanity checks rather than evidence that the teacher matches human judgment.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The evidence supports three claims. First, structured tabular students
are effective distillation targets for this teacher on this benchmark. Model B
already improves substantially over the best CNN and the mean baseline in four
of five affordances, and Model D improves further. Second, the indicator
channel provides useful supervision beyond the scalar score, especially on
L091 Computer Work where Model B is worse than the mean baseline but Model D
recovers to RMSE 0.812 and Pearson $r=0.595$. Third, the strength of indicator
distillation is heterogeneous. On L059, L079, and L091 the random-indicator
control collapses back toward Model B, whereas on L130 and L141 some of the
gain appears to come from the richer sparse representation itself.

For practical use, the results support deployment only in a narrow sense.
Model D is strong enough to act as a cheap front-end for bulk screening,
retrieval, or comparison of design alternatives, where occasional score error
is acceptable and ambiguous cases can be escalated. The results are not strong
enough to justify replacing the VLM teacher outright for image-level analysis.
That is the role we envision inside Image\_Tagger\_3.5, which currently
extracts design-relevant visual, spatial, and cognitive attributes from
interior images: affordance prediction would be added as another fast analysis
layer for cognitive-science and architectural-design workflows, not a final
oracle. The threshold sweep reinforces that caution. If one uses the proper
semantic threshold of 4, the binary task is harder and the gap between student
approximation and categorical judgment becomes more visible. A lower threshold
can improve teacher-matching metrics partly by making the classification
problem easier.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Our primary limitation is label validity. The target variable in every
experiment is a VLM-derived proxy, not a human affordance judgment. As a
result, the reported RMSE, MAE, correlation, and significance tests measure
\emph{distillation fidelity} rather than affordance accuracy in the world. The
first priority for future work is therefore human validation on at least a
subset of scenes. Without that step, the present paper should be read as a
study of teacher approximation, not of ground-truth affordance prediction.

The second limitation is domain. All images come from Hypersim, a photorealistic
but synthetic dataset. This helps control scene coverage and geometry, but it
leaves open the domain gap to real indoor imagery. The segmentation-based
feature pipeline may also behave differently on real photographs.

The third limitation is statistical power. Each affordance has only 84 test
samples, so confidence intervals remain fairly wide and the study is underpowered
for small effects. This is visible in L141, where the point estimate favors
Model D but the paired test is not significant. The fourth limitation is the
uneven reproducibility of retained artifacts: per-image predictions were
available for the reconstructed tabular models, but only aggregate checkpoint
summaries were retained for the CNNs. We therefore report CNN comparisons
transparently as point estimates and avoid unsupported significance claims.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Using 420 synthetic indoor scenes with scene-level splits, lightweight tabular
students can approximate Qwen2-VL-7B's affordance scoring behavior better than
image-only CNN baselines and simple non-neural baselines. Indicator
distillation improves every affordance and is statistically reliable on four of
